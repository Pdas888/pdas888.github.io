About Me

Hi! I’m a final-year PhD candidate at The University of New South Wales, graduating around mid-2026. I study Type Ia supernova remnants, using optical integral-field spectroscopy and modeling to explore the physical and chemical structure of stellar explosions. My main goal is to solve a long-standing question in stellar astrophysics: how do carbon–oxygen white dwarfs explode?

One highlight of my research was capturing the first direct photographic evidence of the double-detonation explosion mechanism in the remnant of SNR 0509-67.5. This work was featured in an ESO press release and covered by major news outlets, earning an Altmetric score of 807.

I’m also interested in developing Integral Field Unit (IFU) data analysis tool and a wide range of observational astronomy, especially systems that involve spectroscopy like pulsar wind nebulae and core-collapse supernova remnants. On the theoretical side, I’ve explored how dark matter can stabilize wormholes and studied black hole analogues in Bose–Einstein condensates to understand their properties and Hawking radiation.

My journey has taken me beyond UNSW Canberra: in 2024, I spent 3 months in Germany working with the Physics of Stellar Objects group under Prof. Dr. Friedrich Röpke. Back in Australia, I currently work in the group of Prof. Dr. Brian Schmidt.

When I’m not doing research, I’m usually playing soccer, enjoying video games, or watching movies. I also love mentoring students, supporting lab demonstrations at UNSW, and helping with support classes.
